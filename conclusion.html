India At-a-Glance: how the Earth's fastest growing economy fairing against the world

With 1.2 billion people and the world’s third-largest economy in purchasing power parity terms, India’s recent growth has been a significant achievement. Since independence in 1947, a landmark agricultural revolution has transformed the nation from chronic dependence on grain imports into an agricultural powerhouse that is now a net exporter of food.

Since independence in 1947, India has been on a remarkable development journey. Today, this once low-income nation has attained lower middle-income status and is home to over 1.3 billion people and a nearly $3 trillion economy. The country has also graduated from being a major recipient of concessional lending to emerge as a donor in its own right.
Below is a timeline of events that showcase some of the major landmarks.

1940-1960:

  • 1945: India, then still under colonial rule, became one of the World Bank’s founding members. Later, it was India that first suggested the creation of a special body to aid developing nations, an idea that bore fruit with the establishment of International Development Association.
  • 1949: Not long after the country gained Independence in 1947, India took its first World Bank loan - for the development of the Indian Railways – one of the largest such networks in the world. This was the World Bank’s first loan to an Asian country and the first to be signed by a woman, Mrs. Vijaya Lakshmi Pandit.
  • 1950: As India began laying the foundations for a modern industrial state, India set up its first power and steel plants, which were often called the "Temples of Modern India".
  • 1960: After successive droughts and major food shortages, India came back and set the stage for its landmark Green Revolution. Projects helped bring more land under cultivation.

    • 1970-1990:

  • 1970: India intensified its focus on agriculture, increased the availability of high-yielding seeds and fertilizers and supported the Food Corporation of India in building facilities to store food grains.
  • 1980: Following global oil shocks, India sought indigenous sources of energy, and developed oil and gas extraction facilities at Bombay High and the Cambay Basin, started exploration of hydrocarbon reserves in the Krishna Godavari Delta.
  • 1990: India launched pathbreaking economic reforms, built institutions for managing critical sectors of a newly globalizing economy. These included PowerGrid, India’s national power transmission utility, which has since emerged as one of the world’s largest such institutions, and the National Highway Authority of India that has transformed highway construction across the country.
    It reformed in rural water supply. Projects also helped eradicate leprosy, reduce cataract blindness, combat polio, malaria and tuberculosis, and improve the nutrition of women and children. India has been polio free since 2014.

    • 2000-2020:

  • 2000: India launched a massive drive to bring every child into school. Pushed to bring an all-weather road to every village. With its outlined vision for inclusive growth, India pioneered a unique approach for improving rural livelihoods - by empowering poor rural women. It took pivotal projects for upgrading the suburban rail infrastructure and build back better program after shattering earthquakes and devastating Tsunami.
  • 2010: Next generation of mega projects were outlined. India moved to greener growth path. Electrified rail freight corridor, shift to multimodal transportation networks, becoming global lead on Solar Energy were among many notable.
  • 2020: India contonued on its path to build a sustainable and resilient economy. When Covid-19 unleashed a crisis of unprecedented magnitude, India managed its own immediate health needs, developed indeginious COVID vaccine, and supported many neighbouring countries with vaccine, PPE and food.
